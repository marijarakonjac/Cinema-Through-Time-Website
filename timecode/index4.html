World War II: Propaganda and the Triumph of Good vs. Evil

World War II solidified cinema's role as a powerful propaganda tool. Governments used film to rally support, inspire hope, and demonize enemies. Hollywood, in particular, became a hub for patriotic storytelling, producing classics such as Casablanca (1942) and The Great Dictator (1940). To see this projection over the war years we started by plotting the different genres comparing them to the Non-War period (1920-1938) and with the average of genres overall on our Dataset. For a broader perspective we plotted the amount of movies per genre (frequency) as well as the percentage each genre occupied with respect to all genres. We applied a log scale for better visualization.

War genre

Perhaps the most obvious choice, we wanted to show how the war genre behaved at this specific point in time and indeed we can see that our analysis reveals a noticeable increase in the production of war movies during World War II, highlighting the genre's heightened relevance at the time. Across the entire dataset, war movies make up 3.57% of all films. However, during World War I (1914–1918), they accounted for only 1.33%, while in World War II (1939–1945), this figure rose significantly to 11.60%. In contrast, during the non war period (1920–1938), war films only made up 2.51% of the total.

Analyzing the relationship between WWII and various genres reveals distinct trends. Crime movies saw a gradual rise leading up to WWII, with a noticeable peak right after the war ended. Although the percentage increase during WWII (4.19%) was modest compared to the entire dataset (4.03%), it still exceeded that of non-war periods (2.13%). Mystery movies showed a clearer connection, with production percentages significantly increasing during WWII (8.13%) compared to WWI (0.73%) and non-war periods (4.72%), supported by a strong correlation (~0.6), indicating the war period's influence on the genre. Similarly, documentaries experienced a spike during WWII (3.98%), higher than both WWI (0.47%) and non-war periods (1.11%), with a moderate correlation (0.5), highlighting the war's impact on this genre. Action movies exhibited a distinct surge during WWII (13.11%), surpassing both WWI (1.33%) and non-war periods (7.82%), although they eventually grew beyond WWII levels over time. Adventure movies followed a different pattern, maintaining high percentages during both war periods, with a significant share during WWII (8.13%) compared to WWI (2.67%) and non-war periods (7.20%), indicating a steady but notable connection to the war period. These trends underline WWII's varying degrees of influence on different film genres, shaping their production in unique ways.

The Space Race: A Leap into the Future

The mid-20th century brought tension all over the world, with the Space Race between the U.S. and the Soviet Union and with it, brought a surge in science fiction films. Movies like 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968) and Star Wars (1977) reflected society's fascination with the possibilities of space exploration. Because of this, we concentrated on the genre of Sci-Fi in this case.

Trend lines

To analyze the behavior of Sci-Fi movies during the space race, we employed linear regression to quantify the trend in their production. Linear regression is a valuable tool for this analysis as it helps identify and measure the rate of change over time, providing a clearer understanding of whether the space race had a noticeable impact on the genre. By focusing on the period immediately preceding the space race and its early years (1943–1959), we aimed to capture how external events might have influenced the Sci-Fi genre.

The regression analysis produced a slope of approximately 0.36, indicating a steady upward trend in the production of Sci-Fi movies during this period. This positive slope suggests that as interest in space exploration grew, so did the prevalence of Sci-Fi films, reflecting how cultural and scientific developments likely influenced the genre. The regression results allow us to draw a more precise connection between the space race and the evolution of Sci-Fi cinema, highlighting the importance of quantitative methods in understanding historical influences on film.

As we can see, a linear regression doesn't match the behaviour of the movie releases during the Space Race period. Therefore, we decided to do a Polynomial regression. As we can see in the graph below, this better fits the percentages of Sci-Fi movies released some years before the Space Race and the Space Race period itself.

For the next analysis, we chose to focus on the event of 9/11, a significant historical moment that has inspired numerous films and documentaries. Given its profound impact, we will concentrate specifically on the documentary genre.

To better understand the influence of 9/11, we will first examine the global trends in documentary production over time. Following this, we will narrow our focus to analyze the behavior of this genre specifically within the United States. This approach will allow us to explore both the worldwide and localized impact of the event on documentary filmmaking.

We can effectively see that, after 9/11, documentaries experienced a very noticeable increase in percentage in both the world and in the United States, we can also see that the plot has the same behaviour but if we see closely the percentage amount in the United States is considerably larger than in the world, it goes up to around 40% where in the world around 28% in its peak.

Trend lines

As with the Space Race analysis, we applied both a linear regression and a polynomial regression to examine the trends. The linear regression from 1990 to 2009 yielded a slope of 0.541, indicating a steady increase in documentary movies related to 9/11. However, the polynomial regression provided a more precise fit, with the function:

f(x) = 187818.534 + -188.447*x^1 + 0.047*x^2

better capturing the fluctuations in the data over time. This suggests that the polynomial model more accurately reflects the changing dynamics in the production of documentary films related to the 9/11 event.


Sentiment analysis

The sentiment analysis conducted on movie summaries over time reveals a clear trend in the emotional tone of films. Prior to 1910, sentiment data shows some noise, but after this period, movies generally exhibited a positive sentiment. However, over time, this sentiment became more negative, stabilizing around zero in the 2010s. The sentiment scores for the entire dataset show a mean of -0.198, with a standard deviation of 0.798, indicating a mix of positive and negative sentiments across films.

The analysis also identifies how significant historical events impacted the sentiment of movies, particularly during wartime. For example, during World War I, there was a notable shift in sentiment, with a significant decrease in sentiment from before to during the war. World War II saw a further decline in sentiment, with significant changes before, during, and after the war. The sentiment scores for WWII show that before the war, sentiment was slightly negative, but it dropped sharply during and even further after the war, with statistically significant changes in sentiment across all periods.

Our analysis of these historical periods shows how filmmakers adapt their stories to reflect the sentiments, fears, and aspirations of the times. By looking at trends in genre distribution and thematic focus, we identify clear patterns of how cinema evolves in response to global events, providing audiences with both a means to process reality and a way to dream of brighter futures.
